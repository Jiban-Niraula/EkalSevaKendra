Abstract

Research Overview

This research examines Ekal Seva Kendra (ESK) — literally "Single Service Center" — a transformative e-governance initiative implemented in Haryana, India, under the National e-Governance Plan (NeGP). ESK consolidated fragmented government service delivery into a unified, citizen-centric single-window model, fundamentally reengineering how public services are accessed, processed, and delivered.

The study analyzes ESK's design philosophy, operational process flow, implementation scale, and measurable outcomes across dimensions of efficiency, corruption reduction, time savings, financial sustainability, and employment creation. It further draws a comparative analysis with Nepal's emerging e-governance infrastructure — particularly the Nagarik App — to identify lessons and policy recommendations for inclusive digital governance in Nepal's context.

The central finding is that ESK's success was not the result of digitizing existing processes, but rather a fundamental redesign of governance around the citizen — a distinction that carries significant implications for countries like Nepal currently building digital public infrastructure.

Section 01 — Introduction & Background

The Problem ESK Was Built to Solve

Before ESK, citizens navigating government services in Haryana faced a fragmented, opaque, and time-consuming bureaucratic system that favoured intermediaries over citizens.

Before ESK

Department-Centric Model

  • Multiple separate offices for different services
  • Repeated paperwork and form submissions per department
  • Long queues and unpredictable waiting times
  • High dependence on middlemen (touts/agents)
  • No transparency in application status
  • High transaction cost in time, money, and effort for citizens
  • Physical file movement prone to loss, manipulation, and delay
After ESK

Citizen-Centric Single-Window

  • One center — one submission — one process
  • Digital routing to relevant departments
  • Time-bound delivery with predictable timelines
  • No intermediaries; direct government-to-citizen flow
  • Trackable applications and digital audit logs
  • Reduced friction and cost for all citizens
  • Physical file movement replaced by digital workflow

Origin & Policy Context

ESK was introduced in Haryana, India as part of India's National e-Governance Plan (NeGP) — a national initiative to modernize government service delivery through digital infrastructure. In Haryana, this manifested through the e-DISHA network and the broader Common Service Centre (CSC) framework under CSC e-Governance Services India Ltd.

The ESK model represents a G2C (Government-to-Citizen) e-governance approach — where the government adapts its systems to serve citizens rather than requiring citizens to navigate complex bureaucratic structures.

Section 02 — Service Delivery Process

How ESK Works — Process Flow

The 7-step ESK process replaces multi-office, multi-visit bureaucracy with a streamlined digital workflow — citizen comes once, and the system does the rest.

01
Citizen Visits ESK
Arrives at a single ESK center — one location for all services.
02
Submits Application
One standardized form, one submission — no department-hopping.
03
Staff Digitizes
Trained operator enters data into the integrated system; paper ends here.
04
System Routes
Application directed digitally to the relevant department — no physical file movement.
05
Department Processes
Internal approval and verification within a defined, time-bound window.
06
Output Returned
Completed output routed back through the same ESK center digitally.
07
Citizen Receives Service
Certificate or document delivered. One visit, fully resolved.
Key Transformation: Physical file movement was replaced by digital workflow movement — reducing delays, errors, and opportunities for corruption. Citizens no longer chase files across departments; the system moves instead.

Section 04 — Outcomes & Impact

What ESK Achieved

The results were measurable, documented, and significant across multiple dimensions of governance quality and citizen experience.

49,627

⚡ Efficiency

Services became electronically issued. Paper dependency and repeated paperwork reduced significantly across all service categories.

⏱ Time

Workflows became time-bound. Many services delivered the same day with predictable digital routing — no more indefinite waiting.

🔒 Corruption

Middlemen dependence reduced. Digital logs and fixed counters limited administrative discretion and opportunities for manipulation.

💰 Finance

Self-sustaining through service fees. Low recurring dependency on government budget made the model replicable and scalable.

👥 Employment

Created local ICT-based jobs at the district level — service operators, data entry roles, and digital support positions.

Section 06 — Lessons Learned

Six Lessons from the ESK Model

Beyond its data, ESK offers a set of transferable principles for any government attempting to modernize citizen service delivery.

1

Redesign, Not Just Digitize

ESK's success was not simply digitizing existing forms. It re-engineered service delivery around the citizen rather than the department. The form changed; so did the logic.

2

Assisted Access Matters

Low digital literacy and rural geography require physical centers with trained staff. Technology alone cannot bridge the access gap — the human interface remains critical.

3

Integration Creates Value

Connecting multiple departments into one backend system multiplies efficiency gains and reduces citizen burden exponentially. Silos kill the single-window promise.

4

Accountability Through Digitization

Digital logs, trackable workflows, and standardized processes reduced discretion and created automatic accountability mechanisms that deter corrupt behavior.

5

Scale for Real Impact

ESK worked because it scaled to 117+ centers covering 100% of rural Haryana. Partial rollouts fail to deliver systemic change — coverage is not optional, it is the point.

6

Self-Sustainability Ensures Longevity

The service-fee model reduced dependence on recurring government funding, making the system financially viable and replicable across other states and countries.

Section 07 — Conclusion

The Bigger Picture

ESK represents more than an IT project — it represents a philosophy of governance that places citizens at the center of public service design.

"Ekal Seva Kendra proved that successful e-governance is not just about digitizing government. It is about redesigning governance around citizens."
Fewer Touchpoints Citizens visit once — not many offices across the city.
Faster Delivery Time-bound, predictable digital workflows replaced indefinite waits.
Lower Citizen Cost Reduced effort, no middlemen, no repeated paperwork.
Reduced Corruption Digital logs and fixed counters create automatic accountability.
Improved Transparency Trackable applications restore citizen trust in public institutions.
